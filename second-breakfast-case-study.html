Where It Started
Built for one person. That person was me.

This didn't start as a product. It started as a personal problem I couldn't find a good answer to.

I had a rough budget for a 6–9 month trip across multiple countries. Not a strict limit, more a sense of what I could spend before coming home in a position I didn't want to be in. I didn't need to track every penny. I needed to know, roughly, whether I was on track to make the money last, and to catch it early if I wasn't. A few months in, somewhere in Southeast Asia, the only honest answer lived in my bank statements, and I hadn't crossed the world to do monthly accounting.

I went looking for an app and gave up before installing one. Account walls, subscriptions, pricing that made no sense for one long trip. Everything I could see was designed for commuters tracking coffee, not travellers crossing borders, and all of it wanted me to sit down at the end of the day and do admin. Evaluating them properly looked like more work than building the thing I actually wanted.

The question I kept coming back to was simpler than any of them were solving: am I burning through this too fast? Weekly. Country by country. Without sitting down to do maths.

I built Second Breakfast to answer that question. Whether it's useful to anyone else is still an open question.

The Problem
Existing tools treat travel like a spreadsheet. Travellers don't.

Long-term travel finance is a genuinely unsolved problem. The category assumes reliable internet, a fixed home currency, and expenses that happen in tidy daily increments. None of those things are true on a trip that runs from Southeast Asia through India and the Himalayas and on to the Camino.

The predictable frictions: logging designed as admin. Multi-currency trips requiring constant manual conversion. Apps that required an account, a subscription, and a cloud connection just to note a chai. And no way to see whether your runway was holding without doing the maths yourself.

The multiday accommodation problem, a 12-night retreat appearing as a catastrophic spike on day one, was discovered during the build rather than before it. But it's a real illustration of a broader issue: these tools aren't built around how travel money actually moves.

📊
What existing apps get wrong
Visible before you even install: daily-tracking design, form-first input, mandatory accounts and cloud sync, multi-currency locked behind premium tiers, anxiety-inducing dashboards. Built for commuters, not travellers.
✍️
What this needed to do
Log it fast, mid-stride, like sending a message. Show the weekly picture without asking for it. Work offline, always. Handle every currency without fuss. And answer the one question that actually matters: am I on track?
Design Principles
Established before a line of code was written.

Four principles governed every feature, copy, and visual decision throughout the build.

Principle 1
Rearview mirror, not speed camera
The app records what happened. It does not warn, nudge, or alarm. If you spent more than your budget this week, the number turns amber. It does not send a notification. It does not suggest you eat less. You are an adult on an adventure.
Principle 2
Offline forever, not offline first
No server. No account. No sync. The app is a single HTML file that runs entirely in the browser, and the data never leaves the device: expenses live in localStorage and back up by CSV export. If the user loses the file, that's on them, and the app tells them this plainly. The one concession to the internet is optional: a tap fetches a live ECB mid-market rate when there's signal, and a nudge appears once the stored rate is more than a week old. No connection? Enter the rate by hand and carry on.
Principle 3
The ledger records payment. The budget tracks value.
A 12-night hotel stay costs money on day one but provides value across 12 nights. These are different things. The ledger shows when you paid. The budget shows the daily cost of what you're getting. This distinction, realised mid-build, required rearchitecting how every calculation worked.
Principle 4
Write it how you'd say it
The input is a freetext field: "chai varanasi 30" or "hotel kovalam 4500 5 nights". An NLP parser extracts amount, duration, category, and location. No tapping through dropdowns. No category selection before you know what you spent. Log it mid-stride, sort it later.
Design Process
Iterative. Constraint-driven. Test-first.

The project ran across multiple sessions using AI-assisted development: the human directing product decisions, brand voice, and UX priorities; the AI handling implementation, testing, and iteration. Roles were kept deliberately separate: product thinking first, then build, then refine.

Origin
A personal problem, not a product brief
Started a few months into a long trip, in Varkala on the Indian coast, from a genuine frustration with the tools on offer. No market research. No validation. Just one question: what would actually work for me? The constraint of building something personal turned out to produce clearer decisions than a hypothetical product would have.
Phase 1 · Foundation
Core architecture & NLP parser
Single-file constraint established. localStorage schema designed. NLP parser built to extract amount, category, location, and duration from natural language. 40+ currencies mapped with auto-flag and auto-symbol lookup. Light/dark theme system. The logging friction was the first problem: "chai varanasi 30" as the entire entry.
Phase 2 · The Spread Problem
Multi-night cost distribution, discovered mid-build
Identified during construction that paying upfront for multi-night accommodation caused false budget spikes. Not the original problem, but a real one. Designed and implemented dailyAud(), dailyAudForWeek(), and attributedToDate(): three helper functions that distribute cost across nights while keeping the ledger as a payment record. Extended allDays() to generate future dates for active stays so the full spread appears immediately.
Phase 3 · Runway View
Trail & weekly visibility
Built the Trail view around the core question: am I on track? Daily spend chart (spread-aware), weekly vs budget bars, By Country breakdown, By Category with % of trip. Designed to answer "how am I doing across this whole trip" without requiring any manual grouping or maths.
Phase 4 · UX Refinement
Confirm card, onboarding, edge cases
Redesigned the confirm card to show the NLP parse prominently with a "not right?" escape hatch, reducing friction for correct entries while keeping correction accessible. Fixed NLP to not force Accommodation for entries with days alone. Added isNight flag to correctly label nights vs days in ledger. Capped Recent at 5 with "See all →" to keep input always visible. Budgeting itself got the biggest rethink: a couple of friends with different kinds of trip broke the single weekly-pot assumption, so onboarding now opens with a choice of four modes. Weekly, monthly, a total pot for the whole trip, or no budget at all, just the record.
Phase 5 · Brand & Voice
Hobbit register. Understated. Proper.
Established brand voice through iteration: early drafts drifted clinical and were course-corrected back to warmth. Key vocabulary: purse, ledger, trail, provisions, accounted for. Built animated screen demo (phone shell, 78-second loop), video storyboard, CapCut overlay cards. Copy went through multiple rounds to stay honest to the origin story rather than pitching a product that didn't start as one.
Phase 6 · Hardening
Full audit, PWA, data safety
76-test audit suite covering NLP parsing, date normalisation, CSV import/export, daily spread, weekly spread, attributed averages, and feature presence. All 76 passing. Added PWA manifest and Apple touch icon for home screen install. Export nudge in ledger after 10+ expenses, weekly and dismissible, because offline-only data needs the user to protect it.
Technical Decisions
Every constraint was a choice.
Architecture
Single HTML file, zero dependencies
No build step. No npm. No framework. The app is a file you can AirDrop, email, or download. It runs identically in 2026 as it will in 2036. Intentional constraint that shaped every subsequent decision, and the right one for a traveller on a bus in Vietnam with patchy wifi.
Data Model
Expense objects store the moment of payment
Each expense records the original local-currency amount, the home-currency equivalent at that day's rate, the rate itself, and the country and currency context at the moment of entry. Historical expenses stay correct after every border crossing and rate change. The internal field names still read inr and aud: the app was born on the India leg, home currency in Australian dollars, and never pretended otherwise.
Spread Logic
Three layers of calculation, one source of truth
dailyAud(e, date): single day share for a single expense.
dailyAudForWeek(e, weekNum): weekly share for the budget widget.
attributedToDate(d): running total for avg/day calculation.
Ledger and trip total always use raw aud. Budget, chart, and avg/day always use spread helpers. The distinction is enforced architecturally.
NLP Parser
Nights-first extraction prevents amount contamination
Early bug: "hotel 45000 3 nights" parsed 3 as the amount. Fix: extract the nights/days count first, remove it from the number pool, then take the largest remaining number as the amount. Supports k-suffix (1.5k → 1500). Category scoring uses keyword weight: longer keywords score higher to prevent false matches. Corrections persist: recategorise a word once and it files itself correctly from then on.

Brand Voice
Hobbit-register. Understated. Proper.

The brand voice is not the Hobbit IP. It is the sensibility: an unhurried traveller who takes their comforts seriously, accounts for everything properly, and has no intention of rushing. The tone emerged through iteration: early drafts drifted clinical, were course-corrected back to warmth.

Vocabulary in use: purse, ledger, trail, provisions, accounted for, proper, settled, sorted, noted.
Vocabulary avoided: budget tracker, expense app, NLP, smart, real-time, sync, dashboard.
